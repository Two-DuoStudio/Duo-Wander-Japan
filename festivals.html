Where Traditions Come Alive in Celebration

Obon

Obon: A Dance of Spirits and Light

✦ One of Japan's most important Buddhist traditions, held in mid-August
✦ Dedicated to honoring the spirits of ancestors returning to the world of the living
✦ Families gather to clean graves, light lanterns, and offer prayers
✦ Bon Odori dances are performed in town squares and temple grounds
✦ Lanterns are placed at doorways to guide ancestral spirits home
✦ At the end, lanterns are floated on rivers or the sea to send spirits back
✦ Each region has its own unique style of dance, music, and costumes
✦ Streets are filled with food stalls, paper lanterns, and festival games
✦ Some towns host large-scale fireworks to mark the occasion
✦ Families reunite, share meals, and strengthen generational bonds
✦ Obon reflects Japan's deep respect for ancestors and the spiritual world
✦ A time of remembrance, joy, and connection across all of Japan

Setsubun

Setsubun: Festival of Fortune

✦ Celebrated on the day before the start of spring in the traditional calendar
✦ Known as the “Bean-Throwing Festival” across Japan
✦ Families scatter roasted soybeans to chase away evil spirits
✦ Shouting “Oni wa soto! Fuku wa uchi!” meaning “Demons out! Luck in!”
✦ People eat the same number of beans as their age for good health
✦ Temples and shrines host large ceremonies with bean-throwing events
✦ In some places, celebrities or sumo wrestlers throw beans to the crowd
✦ Children wear oni (demon) masks while others throw beans at them
✦ Special lucky sushi rolls called ehōmaki are eaten facing a lucky direction
✦ Paper charms and decorations are used to protect homes
✦ Regional customs add local twists, such as throwing peanuts instead of beans
✦ Setsubun marks the symbolic driving away of misfortune
✦ A joyful, family-centered celebration welcoming spring and fortune

Tanabata

Tanabata: Festival of Stars

✦ Celebrated across Japan on July 7th, though some regions hold it in August
✦ Based on the Chinese legend of Orihime and Hikoboshi, lovers separated by the Milky Way
✦ It is said they can only meet once a year, on the night of Tanabata
✦ People write wishes on colorful strips of paper called tanzaku
✦ Wishes are hung on bamboo branches decorated with ornaments
✦ Streets and shopping arcades are filled with streamers and paper decorations
✦ Sendai's Tanabata Festival is the most famous, attracting thousands of visitors
✦ Brightly colored streamers symbolize weaving threads of Orihime
✦ Parades, dances, and fireworks often accompany the celebrations
✦ Some regions float decorated bamboo into rivers or burn them after the festival
✦ Seasonal foods like somen noodles are enjoyed, representing flowing stars
✦ A romantic, colorful celebration blending legend, art, and community spirit

Hanami

Hanami: Blossoms of Togetherness

✦ Hanami means “flower viewing,” mostly cherry blossoms
✦ Celebrated in spring, usually March to April
✦ Families, friends, and coworkers gather under blooming trees
✦ Popular spots include parks, temples, and riversides
✦ People spread mats for picnics under the blossoms
✦ Food and drinks like bento, sake, and sweets are shared
✦ Nighttime hanami is called yozakura (night cherry blossoms)
✦ Lanterns and lights create a magical evening glow
✦ Symbolizes the fleeting beauty of life
✦ Started centuries ago, once only for nobles
✦ Now a national event celebrated across Japan
✦ Combines nature, culture, and togetherness in one tradition

Sumidagawa Fireworks

Sumidagawa Fireworks: Tokyo's Night

✦ One of Japan's oldest and most famous fireworks festivals, held annually in Tokyo
✦ Dates back to 1733, originally to honor victims of floods and fires
✦ Takes place along the Sumida River, with viewing from both riverbanks
✦ Attracts over a million spectators each year
✦ Features hundreds of fireworks launched in synchronized displays
✦ Traditional music often accompanies the fireworks for a festive atmosphere
✦ Competitions between pyrotechnicians showcase innovative firework designs
✦ Many spectators wear yukata to enjoy the festival in traditional style
✦ Street vendors sell snacks, drinks, and souvenirs along the river
✦ The festival lasts several hours, often with a dramatic grand finale
✦ Nighttime of Tokyo's skyline combined with fireworks create stunning visuals
✦ A summer highlight that celebrates Japanese culture, and community spirit

Shichi-Go-San

Shichi-Go-San: Children's Festival

✦ Celebrated nationwide on November 15th to pray for children's healthy growth
✦ The name literally means “Seven-Five-Three” representing the ages celebrated
✦ Girls aged 3 and 7 and boys aged 3 and 5 participate
✦ Children dress in traditional kimono or hakama for the occasion
✦ Families visit Shinto shrines to offer prayers and blessings
✦ Children receive special candies called chitose-ame, symbolizing longevity
✦ Colorful bags with cranes and turtles are often given as gifts
✦ Photography is popular; many families capture the memorable day
✦ Local shrines hold ceremonies with priests offering blessings
✦ The festival emphasizes gratitude, health, and happiness for children
✦ Traditions have been practiced for hundreds of years across Japan
✦ A beautiful blend of family, culture, and Shinto rituals
